# Purchasing power calculator: when salary and rent sit in different currencies

A job offer lands. The headline number is in **euros** or **dollars**, the new city’s rent is googled in **local units**, and the banking app shows an exchange rate that already feels different a month later. Without a second thread — **price-adjusted** macro anchors from the **country profiles** — teams often negotiate only half the story.

The NationFiles **purchasing power calculator** picks up there. It uses the same **published** macro context as the **Purchasing Power Index** on country cards: **GDP at purchasing power parity per capita** as the cross-country comparator, plus companion indicators wired on each profile. **International HR** and **expats** get figures that line up with tables analysts already cite, not a parallel “secret” index.

## Local costs: enter them deliberately

The calculator is **not** payroll software. It does not infer social-security caps, visa-specific deductions, or equity programmes unless you account for them elsewhere. **Rent, tuition, commuting** go in as **explicit** lines. Skip a row and the chart tells a **partial** story on purpose — compensation committees should **see** the gap before policy freezes.

## Exchange rates and PPP: two speeds

**Naciro** hydrates **high-refresh** FX ribbons on NationFiles. They explain why **nominal** amounts **jump** while **PPP** narratives in macro data move **slowly**. The calculator places **both** readings side by side: the **operational** rate view and the **structural** parity view. It does **not** claim that an FX feed **redefines** World Bank-style methodology.

## When the tool is enough — and when it is not

Use it for **offer talks**, **allowance reviews**, and **executive one-pagers**. Do not treat it as a **visa** evidence pack, **employer-of-record** checklist, or **investment** memo. Automated republication belongs under the [AI guidelines](https://nationfiles.com/en/ai-guidelines/) with **HTTPS** citations.
